Whitehall & Company

A boutique investment bank located in the heart of New York's Times Square

Project Timeline: November 2010 — February 2011

the mission

Whitehall's existing website was a static HTML site that Jon, one of the Managing Directors, had created in his spare time. Because Jon designed it himself he was understandably attached to the appearance of the site, but was finding it prohibitively time consuming to maintain its development. Jon wanted to create a system that would allow employees with little to no knowledge of web coding languages to easily publish site content and assist with maintaining the site on a day-to-day basis.

the strategy

For this project I proposed that Whitehall convert its existing static, hard-coded website to a database-driven object-oriented content management system (CMS) that would deliver content dynamically from a central database. The convenience and scalability provided by content management systems would fulfill their requirement for an easy-to-use yet powerful tool for effortlessly maintaining an active online presence.

 

the hurdle

Because of my previous experience developing and administering complex CMS-based community websites I was confident that I would be able to exceed Whitehall's expectations. The only real challenge I anticipated was the capability to preserve the full integrity of the original site's layout and design. Leading up to this project my chief CMS implementation technique was to install a prepackaged template to create the layout and then adjust the CSS code in order to create the desired ambiance, but because Whitehall wanted to create a customized layout this would not be an option.

the execution

I chose to use Joomla CMS in the implementation of this project because of its extensible feature set and flexible structure. I created a custom PHP template from scratch using Joomla's proprietary framework, successfully preserving the layout and design of the original website while integrating the powerful Joomla content management features. Once the layout was complete I developed custom modules to automatically integrate publication of the firm's weekly newsletter, Monitoring the Markets, as well as add additional dynamic elements to support greater interactivity, better search engine optimization (SEO), faster page-loading, and a cleaner layout.

 

the result

The entire Whitehall team was highly impressed by the ease of use provided by the content management system for publishing information and the improved site structure contributed by the custom template. The weekly market update newsletter that I also developed using Microsoft Visual Basic and Bloomberg APIs is sent to over 1,000 investors and generates a considerable amount of traffic to the site.

LoSo/Tap the Campus

A location-based recommendation, notification, and rewards app for restaurants & bars

Project Timeline: June — October 2012

the mission

The first iteration of Tap The Campus (and later LoSo) originated while attending at the University of Delaware, where Frank Trofa and I identified that there was a major communication deficiency between local social venues and their patrons. At this time Twitter, Foursquare, and even Facebook were all still in their infancy but becoming hugely popular on campus, and bar and restaurant owners were not effective at promoting their businesses via these avenues.

the strategy

Frank and I began developing an app that would allow patrons to evaluate what was happening in their area all in one place. Users could check-in to a place and tell their followers what they felt about the current ambiance using logical mood icons. Users could choose to receive notifications containing that night’s specials, what events were going on, and other relevant information from their favorite bars and restaurants. Restaurant owners could manage their page on the centralized network, effortlessly allowing them to stay connected with the decentralized local community.

 

the hurdle

Frank and I had considerable experience creating community websites, but figuring out how to effectively market our application to both restaurateurs and the community at the same time was problematic. It became clear that it would be necessary to quickly gain a critical mass of users and venues concurrently. We needed users to entice restaurant owners, and our users needed to have access to as many bars as possible in order for the service to be useful to them. Additionally, as technology in the location space matured we were faced with fierce competition from other services with overlapping functions and much stronger market share, like Yelp and Foursquare.

the execution

Frank and I were put into contact with Rich, owner of LoSo, after pitching our application to a start-up incubator at the Early Stage East conference in Philadelphia in the Summer of 2012. LoSo was a similar application that had established itself in the local area as the premiere service to receive notifications and earn rewards for visiting local venues. Frank and I joined with Rich to re-launch LoSo after it had become entangled in complications with predatory investors. We worked to overhaul the existing code (written in Symfony II PHP), strip down the existing application and re-launch it with more innovative features. For deploying the app we chose Amazon Web Services (S3, EC2, etc.) to host the database and provide the app with dynamic, elastic storage.

 

the result

We successfully re-developed much of the existing application from scratch, but further work on the project has been suspended until negotiation complications with the group of former investors who retain control of the original LoSo assets and intellectual property has been resolved. While active LoSo had over 8,000 users, all from the original test market of Philadelphia.

UDress Magazine

A student-run bi-yearly fashion publication at the University of Delaware

Project Timeline: April 2008 — May 2010

the mission

UDressmag.com

UDress Magazine recruited me as their Web Chairman after my website for DelRide SnowCorp started gaining attention in the University community. UDress Magazine was interested in building a similar online community for their members, creating a public forum where participants could discuss fashion together and share their favorite trends. They hoped to not only showcase the physical magazine, but also compliment it with editorial articles and exclusive online content.

lookbook.udressmag.com

A second website that I launched for UDress was an exclusive “lookbook” site, where members could post, comment, and rate each other’s outfits (similar to the popular fashion social network lookbook.nu). This website was limited to acting members of the magazine in order to help protect its users, and employed very sophisticated integration to the Facebook API. Unfortunately due to fundamental changes in the Facebook API the website is no longer accessible. Nonetheless, it was an extremely rewarding project to develop.

 

the strategy

I worked closely with the UDress executive team to understand what features they were looking for in an online community website and how they wanted to manage the relationship between the physical magazine and the online entity. Especially important to them was the ability to keep readers interested in the magazine between bi-yearly issues by providing a constant supply of new content to the site. I was provided with a web team to help write content and manage administration of the site.

the hurdle

Because of my experience with DelRide I had an understanding of what would work for UDress. However, since this was going to be collaborative website, as opposed to running the entire site myself, I had to take into account ease of use in the backend. Because of this I decided to scrap Drupal and develop UDress’s website on the Joomla framework, which has a clear administration interface.

 

the execution

I led the web team in the installation and customization of the Joomla configuration, modifying the PHP code of nodes and modules in order to generate the preferred web application functions. I digitalized the magazine for online reading, adapting a Flash plug-in to animate digital page turns. I performed search engine optimization diagnostics and implemented controls to boost search rankings. I structured the backend for automated content creation, designing templates and assisting content editors with creating content for the website.

the result

The new UDress website was very well received. Not only was the website popular among University students, it began attracting a world audience. Just one year after its launch the website was generating an average of over 4,000 page views per month, with traffic coming from more than 130 countries on 6 continents. The framework that I put into place made it so easy to publish new content that editors from other departments began posting their own sections on the website, which has now grown to almost 1,500 pages.

DelRide SnowCorp

A not-for-profit ski & snowboard club for Delaware Valley-area college students

Project Timeline: February 2007 — May 2010

the mission

DelRide SnowCorp was formed in 2001 as a group of University of Delaware students who shared a passion for skiing and snowboarding. I ran for and was elected to the position of Web Chairman as a freshman, the youngest member of the board. I was extremely eager to accept the role and start building web applications to enhance the organization’s membership and involvement.

the strategy

My foremost intention was to create a website where club members could connect… to build an online community that would strengthen member’s relationships with the organization and each other, to encourage member participation and interaction, and to facilitate the rapid delivery of important information. I would provide club members with the ability to create user profiles, upload photos, find, register, and pay for events electronically, and participate in club discussions. I also wanted to boost new member acquisition by using videos, pictures, and activity news to entice non-members to join.

 

the hurdle

Before becoming DelRide’s Web Chair my previous experience was developing websites using HTML and CSS, but I recognized that I would have to utilize advanced web programming techniques like PHP and database scripting in order to create the community network I envisioned. I began studying how to design and develop online communities and immediately became attracted to the power and flexibility that content management systems provided for handling complex relationships amongst large amounts of data.

the execution

I started development on the Drupal CMS framework, coding and installing extensions that used database calls and dynamic page creation techniques to capture, process and deliver information to each user independently. I built an interactive calendar for displaying important club information (upcoming meetings, trips, social events, etc.), created galleries to showcase user’s pictures, curated a message board to allow club members to easily connect with each other, and built a multifaceted system for creating, promoting, and joining carpools to local resorts so members could easily find people to ski and ride with.

 

the result

Almost immediately after the website was launched the club started to see a fundamental shift in its social fabric. The facilitation of online interaction outside of club-sponsored events attributed to the strengthening of the organization’s fellowship. This stronger member engagement resulted in improved event attendance and increased member sign-ups. Existing members who may have felt isolated before were now more comfortable participating in social events, and new members joined because of the club’s increased prominence in the community. Keeping up with club affairs became effortless, and because I established online transaction processing, running and joining club events was easier than ever. Club membership surged from just 40 to over 160 members, and the year after the launch of the new website we expanded to include nearby colleges in our club outings. I was elected to club president after holding the position of web chair for two years, but continued to be closely involved in the enhancement of the site’s features.

Backcountry.com CSBS

Customer Service Bulletin Semi-Weekly digital archive project

Project Timeline: January 2013 — March 2013

the mission

Shortly after joining backcountry.com I recognized that there was an absence of adequate distribution and archiving of the internal company newsletter, the Customer Service Bulletin Semi-Weekly (CSBS). I initiated and led a project to digitalize and present a fully formatted and search-engine-optimized version of the newsletter on the internal company Wiki network.

 

the strategy

I really appreciated the way that Lexi (the newsletter founder) formatted the printed newsletter, succinct and clear for maximum information impact, and I wanted to translate that onto the wiki page. The Customer Service web team suggested that I just display a screenshot, but I wanted the text to be searchable, editable, and clear, so I knew that would not work.

the hurdle

Before undertaking this endeavor I had only minimal experience with Wiki Markup Language (WML), so staying true to the original design’s form was harder than expected considering the limitations of WML. Many times I had to code with no preview because the tabular formatting would break the window until it all came together.

 

the execution

I created an attractive gallery archive page with pictures of each issue that linked to the full text version. After a few iterations, I succeeded at creating a pixel-for-pixel replica of the original printed newsletter so customer service representatives could easily find important information about new products and techniques.

the result

The customer service department supervisors were extremely impressed with my initiative to go above and beyond my job responsibilities, while also maintaining consistent sales quotas, and I received considerable recognition from upper management. I was brought in on PHP-based database statistics projects and was asked to participate in user interface/user experience discussions. The archive is still maintained using my original techniques.

Edgemoor Community Center

A non-profit community center providing daycare and after school care services

Project Timeline: September 2009 — June 2010

the mission

I designed and developed Edgemoor Community Center’s website as part of a team project my senior year of college. Our mission was to produce a website that was not only informative and aesthetically pleasing but also employed cutting-edge usability practices and designs for handicapped and less-technologically-adept individuals.

the strategy

Because this was a childcare facility and community center I wanted to create a site that was simple in design yet visually stimulating. Edgemoor Community Center provided my team with a large amount of information to categorize, so creating a clear navigational hierarchy was key.

 

the hurdle

Although I had never created a Flash animation before, I decided that it would be the best application for Edgemoor’s site. A simple animated header would certainly achieve the balance between “fun” and “simple” without overwhelming users who were on older systems or were not familiar with browsing the internet, as the header would not have an effect on navigation and would depreciate to a static image on slower systems.

the execution

After performing numerous usability studies my team settled on a simple and traditional page layout with an animated header, left-hand navigation, and center body. We wanted to use primary colors, with minimal shading or gradients. Most importantly, we utilized Google apps integration to allow the community center employees to easily update the website after we finished the project.

 

the result

Not only did my team receive an “A” on the project, but the community center hired me on as a contractor after the semester ended. I helped teach the staff how to update the website and they are still using my design.

Light Up The Queen Foundation

A non-profit theatre redevelopment and revitalization project in Wilmington, DE

Project Timeline: February 2009 — April 2009

the mission

I was lead developer for the Light Up The Queen Foundation website as part of a team project my junior year of college. The Queen, a 45,000 square foot theatre in downtown Wilmington Delaware had been abandoned for five decades and the Light Up The Queen Foundation needed a website to inspire community interest in its redevelopment.

the strategy

One of the most striking features of the Queen Theatre was the state in which it had been left. Ancient movie projectors decayed with their last film reels still in place. The decorative wallpaper, once a glimmering red and gold, was peeling from the walls. Rays of light peaked through the ceiling, simultaneously illuminating the structure’s ornate details while exposing its disintegration. We yearned to capture the potential concealed under years of rust and deterioration.

 

the hurdle

This was the first time that I worked exclusively on the development side of a project, which presented me with its own unique set of complications. Collaborating with the design team, film crew, research team and marketing teams meant that concessions would have to be made in order to incorporate all of their desired features while maintaining a functional and cohesive website.

the execution

We developed a website heavily reliant on large, beautiful images to convey the elegance of the building. The Homepage featured a rotating set of images in the background. We incorporated a photo timeline and picture gallery, as well as a short film about the history of the theatre told through the memories of Wilmington residents who had been patrons of the theatre in their youth.

 

the result

The website was an important resource advocating for the redevelopment of the old theatre. WXPN radio station in Philadelphia announced that they would open a branch of their popular music dinner theatre, the World Café Live, in the queen. Wilmington real estate developers Buccini/Pollin Group and city officials soon joined in the initiative to restore the Queen Theater. In October 2009, six months after the launch of the website, the $25 million required for restoration was secured and the Light Up The Queen Foundation announced a Spring 2011 opening. The theatre remains a bastion of hope in the downtown Wilmington revitalization movement.
